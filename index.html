Short Research Statement

I want to understand how optimization selects solutions in highly overparameterized models, and how this selection gives rise to generalization.

The literature on generalization is often split into frameworks that explain particular phenomena in isolation, but do not fit together into a coherent account of modern machine learning. This includes perspectives based on implicit bias toward low-norm solutions, interpolation-based explanations of phenomena such as double descent, and views of generative modeling in which structured errors are essential for good performance. Each of these captures part of the picture, but none seems to explain the full range of behaviors observed in overparameterized systems.

I am interested in developing theoretical frameworks that connect optimization dynamics, model parameterization, and data structure, with the goal of explaining these phenomena within a common picture rather than treating them as separate effects.

Ongoing Research

  • Towards a Causal Understanding of Low-Rank Structure and Generalization in Deep Learning Current literature often treats low effective rank and neural collapse as causes of generalization, arising through the implicit bias of optimization. However, this picture does not account well for settings such as weight interpolation, where effective rank can increase, neural collapse can break, and generalization can still improve.

    In this project, I ask whether low rank is truly causal, or whether it is instead a by-product of optimization converging to simple explanations of the data. My working hypothesis is that the implicit bias of current optimization algorithms is toward such simple solutions, which often coincide with low-rank structure but need not be low rank in general.

    My goal is to formalize this notion of simplicity and disentangle it from low effective rank in order to better understand the causal role of low rank in generalization. More broadly, this could suggest new optimization mechanisms with an implicit or explicit bias toward solutions that generalize well without directly constraining rank, potentially increasing the available degrees of freedom without unnecessarily restricting model expressivity.
  • Correcting the Null Hypothesis of AGN Light Curves using Generative Models Repurposing continuous normalizing flows to learn the correct noise distribution from active galactic nuclei, with the goal of improving detection of exotic supermassive black hole dynamics.

Bio

I am a PhD student in computer science at ISTA and an ELLIS scholar. I completed my BSc and MSc in computer science at the Technion, where I did my master's with Prof. Alex Bronstein on machine learning for scientific applications.

My earlier research focused on generative modeling and probabilistic inference in biology and physics. In particular, I worked on protein structure modeling under experimental constraints, as well as on learning and inference in settings with strong dependencies and non-i.i.d. sampling, where observations are correlated rather than independently drawn. Working on these problems, increasingly pushed me toward more fundamental questions: why modern machine learning models generalize so well, how generative models produce realistic new samples, and what dynamics govern learning in highly overparameterized systems.

Although my research experience so far has been almost entirely applied, working on these problems made me realize that I am far more interested in the fundamental principles that govern learning itself than in using machine learning as a tool for scientific applications. This has led to a deliberate shift in my interests toward training dynamics, implicit bias, and generalization in modern machine learning, with the goal of developing a more mathematically grounded understanding of how optimization, model structure, and data assumptions interact. I am especially interested in being in an environment where I can pursue this transition seriously and learn from strong researchers working on these questions.

Outside of research, I enjoy reading, snowboarding, and surfing. If an activity comes with no risk of breaking a leg, it is probably not worth my time. Except for reading. Reading is awesome.
